Natasha Biscoe et al. prepared a study with the aim of investigating the relationship between executive function and complex PTSD symptoms in UK Armed Forces veterans. During this, with a dataset of veterans diagnosed with complex PTSD, symptom severity was assessed by using the PTSD Symptom Inventory and executive function was measured with tests like the Wisconsin Card Sorting and Stroop Tests. By performing such statistical analyses as regression and correlation, the researchers discovered certain consequences between PTSD symptoms and dysfunctions in executive function. In specific, cognitive fatigue corresponded with poorer cognitive flexibility and avoidance symptoms were tied to the breakdown of judgement and problem-solving. This experiment therefore deduced that complex PTSD has a significant consequence on executive function in veterans, making a case for cognitive rehabilitation as part of their treatment strategies and progressing towards some examination of causal relationships and interventions.

Murphy et al. proposed an article exploring the unique challenges veterans face when seeking mental health support, identifying barriers to care and needed assistance. The dataset consisted of a literature review and qualitative studies, including interviews, surveys, and observational analyses. The findings highlighted significant barriers such as stigma, lack of awareness, and logistical issues, with veterans preferring peer-led support tailored to their experiences. The study concluded that understanding veterans' needs is key to improving mental health services, emphasizing the need for targeted interventions and increased awareness. It called for further research and policy initiatives to ensure comprehensive support for veterans.




Murphy et al. proposed a study investigating the prevalence of ICD-11 PTSD and complex PTSD (CPTSD) among treatment-seeking veterans, focusing on associated risk factors and comorbid conditions. The dataset included veterans diagnosed with PTSD and CPTSD, using clinical interviews and standardized assessments. Descriptive statistics and logistic regression identified prevalence rates, risk factors, and comorbidities. Results showed high prevalence of both PTSD and CPTSD, with risk factors including trauma exposure, prior mental health issues, and demographics. Comorbidity with depression, anxiety, and substance use was common. The study concluded that CPTSD may require distinct treatment approaches and emphasized the need for integrated treatment strategies to address comorbid conditions in veterans.





Reed et al. recommended conducting research on the prevalence of chronic pain and PTSD among U.S. military veterans, with a focus on their mental health effects. Using standardized tests that assessed chronic pain, PTSD symptoms, and functional impairment, the National Health Study included veterans in the dataset. Descriptive statistics and logistic regression were employed to determine prevalence rates and their correlations. The study revealed that individuals with chronic pain and PTSD experienced stronger psychological changes during their younger age. Both conditions resulted in greater functional impairment for veterans compared to individuals with just one condition.

The International Trauma Questionnaire (ITQ) was developed and validated by Cloitre et al. as a self-reporting tool for diagnosing PTSD and complex ADD using ICD-11 criteria. Pilot studies were conducted on populations that were exposed to trauma and utilized the data as well as established PTSD measures. The psychometric features of the ITQ, such as construct validity and diagnostic accuracy, were validated through factor analysis and reliability testing. The ITQ accurately categorized PTSD and CPTSD symptoms, with significant correlations to established benchmarks. In their research, they emphasized the importance of the ITQ as a reliable tool for both cultural reflection and clinical practice, and recommended that it be integrated into clinical settings to aid in the assessment/treatment of PTSD and CPTSD.

Novaco et al. utilized data from the National Vietnam Veterans Readjustment Study (NVVRS) to investigate the link between combat-related PTSD, anger, and violence in veterans. Additionally, A study was conducted on 1,200 male combat veterans and 259 clinical interviews, analyzing their PTSD and anger indices. Their findings suggest that the risk of violence when anger levels are high is likely to be exacerbated by PTSD, which triggers a feedback loop. While violence was not directly linked to PTSD, the study found that anger and compulsion to violence had significant multiplicity effects. Rather, they suggest that in many of the programs for PTSD prevention and treatment, anger is an important intervention target because it helps reduce risk of violence.
